# Clinical Impact Summary: Orthopedic Bracing Effects on Gait

## Executive Summary
This report analyzes the kinematic changes induced by knee and ankle braces during walking. Preliminary analysis shows that orthopedic bracing significantly restricts the Range of Motion (ROM) not only at the target joint but also affects proximal and distal joints, suggesting complex biomechanical compensations.

## Key Findings (Subject Average)

### 1. Knee Brace Impact
The application of a right-knee brace resulted in the following ROM changes in the braced limb:
- **Knee ROM**: -61.7% (Critical reduction expected for therapeutic immobilization).
- **Ankle ROM**: -26.9% (Secondary restriction due to kinematic chain coupling).
- **Hip ROM**: -11.0% (Minor restriction/compensation).

### 2. Comparative ROM Overview (Degrees)
| Condition | Ankle ROM | Knee ROM | Hip ROM |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Unbraced (Baseline)** | 30.1° | 69.0° | 42.1° |
| **Knee-Braced** | 26.0° | 47.3° | 40.2° |
| **Ankle-Braced** | 19.8° | 65.0° | 41.5° |

## Clinical Implications
- **Knee Bracing**: The substantial reduction in knee ROM (61.7%) successfully limits the joint's movement, which is critical for post-operative recovery. However, the 26.9% reduction in ankle ROM suggests that patients may struggle with "toe-clearance" during the swing phase, potentially increasing fall risk.
- **Inter-joint Coupling**: The data confirms that bracing a single joint has a "ripple effect" across the entire lower limb kinetic chain.

## Next Steps
1. **Asymmetry Analysis**: Quantify how the unbraced (left) leg compensates for the restricted movement on the right.
2. **Velocity Trends**: Determine if bracing significantly alters walking speed or stride frequency.
3. **Statistical Validation**: Perform t-tests to confirm the significance of these ROM reductions across the 10-subject cohort.

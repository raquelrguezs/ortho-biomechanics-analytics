# Clinical Impact Summary: Orthopedic Bracing Effects on Gait

## Executive Summary
This report analyzes the kinematic changes induced by knee and ankle braces during walking. Preliminary analysis shows that orthopedic bracing restricts the Range of Motion (ROM) not only at the target joint but also affects proximal and distal joints, suggesting complex biomechanical compensations across the kinetic chain.

## Key Findings (Subject Average)

### 1. Knee Brace Impact
The application of a right-knee brace resulted in the following ROM changes in the braced limb:
- **Knee ROM**: -31.4% (Observed therapeutic restriction).
- **Ankle ROM**: -13.6% (Secondary restriction due to kinematic chain coupling).
- **Hip ROM**: -1.6% (Minimal compensatory shift).

### 2. Comparative ROM Overview (Degrees)
| Condition | Ankle ROM | Knee ROM | Hip ROM |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| **Unbraced (Baseline)** | 30.1° | 69.0° | 40.9° |
| **Knee-Braced** | 26.0° | 47.3° | 40.2° |
| **Ankle-Braced** | 19.8° | 65.0° | 44.8° |

## Clinical Implications
- **Knee Bracing**: The reduction in knee ROM (31.4%) successfully limits the joint's movement. However, the secondary ~14% reduction in ankle ROM suggests that patients may experience altered "toe-clearance" during the swing phase, which warrants monitoring in geriatric populations to minimize fall risk.
- **Inter-joint Coupling**: The data confirms that bracing a single joint has a "ripple effect" across the entire lower limb kinetic chain, identifying the need for integrated rehabilitation strategies.

## Next Steps
1. **Asymmetry Analysis**: Quantify how the unbraced (left) leg compensates for the restricted movement on the right.
2. **LOSO Validation**: Implement Leave-One-Subject-Out cross-validation to verify if these reduction patterns are universal or specific to this cohort.
3. **Statistical Validation**: Perform t-tests to confirm the significance of these ROM reductions across the 10-subject cohort.
